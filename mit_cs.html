Learning MIT's Computer Science Curriculum

The Curriculum

Computer science was always something I had been interested in. I had taken a few free courses on Coursera, but never took the full dive. Having my post-graduate job cancelled due to Covid-19, I decided to turn this setback on its head and start tackling computer science.
I quickly discovered the plethora of resources available, but conversly, found it very difficult to decide which ones to pursue. Stuck in a loop of comparing the seemingly endless available resources, I thought it would be best to pick one to start, and begin making progress. I decided to follow MIT's curriculum for a few reasons. First, I could be confident in the breadth and depth of topics covered. As one of the top engineering universities in the world, I knew their curriculum would be pristine. Second, there were plenty of resources available via their open-courseware platforms (more on this shortly). Most courses had everything from tutorials to exams available. And lastly, the structured curriculumn would prevent me from needing to waste time in planning out and finding the next courses to pursue, because I had a carefully planned roadmap already created for me.
My engineering degree endowed me with the prerequisite math knowledge required for the computer science curriculum. Further, complementary electives in the humanities required for a standard degree would play no role in advancing my computer science knowledge, and I had already taken some in my undergrad. Hence, I decided to focus soley on the third of a degree comprised on the core computer science requirements.

Methodology

MIT has three main branches for their open-courseware resources:

  • edX.org
  • Open Learning Library
  • MIT OCW

The first of these resources, edX, emmulates end-to-end MIT courses with start/end dates, live teaching support, discussion forms, exams, and certifications for courses passed. One the other end of this spectrum is MIT OCW which is completely self-guided but still with all MIT lectures, assignments, labs, exams, each with their respective solutions available. There is not however, a certification available for completion, because nothing is auto-graded. Between these two resources is the Open Learning Library with auto-graded assignments and homeworks. For each course I prioritized edX availability, then OLL and finally MIT OCW, to ensure I am taking advantage of the most ersources offered.
